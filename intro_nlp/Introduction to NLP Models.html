How do machines understand text data?¶

Word embeddings¶

To understand how we start modelling text and language, we first need a comprehensive understanding of how machines understand text data, that is, we need to understand embeddings. Embeddings are essentially representations of objects in a vector space, without going into too much algebra, embeddings convert abstract objects into usable numeric vectors. As an example, word embedding are vector representations of words, sentence embeddings of sentences, and document embeddings of documents. Embeddings aren't unique to natural language processing, we see them all across data science. We have can geographic embeddings e.g. a 1-0 coded vector of where your friend may possibly be on a grid or image embedding which are representations of an images as vectors or tensors.

Mathematically, the embeddings we'll consider are functions $f: X \to \mathbb{R}^n$ where $n$ differs by the type of embedding you choose and $X$ is usually a set of words.

Simple word embeddings¶

We'll use example of 3 words, "I", "like", and "cats". The simplest word embedding is a simple one-hot encoding, that is,

$I = [1,0,0]$

$like = [0,1,0]$

$cats = [0,0,1]$

Text processing¶

We see some obvious issues in the above representations, some main ones are

Some words contain little information

In our example, some of the words have relatively little information such as "I" and "and" these words are called stop words. These can be removed fairly in a fairly standard way.

Similar words have different vector representations

In our simple word embeddings, the word "study", "studies", "studying", "student" will be represented as different vectors, however they all have the same root.

Two simple ways of dealing with this is lemmatisation or stemming. Both are ways of obtaining the root word, the key difference is that a lemmatisation has context built in and will convert words to proper dictionary words while stemming doesn't.

$Original: study, studies, student, good, better, car, automobile$

$Lemmatised: study, study, study, good, good, car, car$

$Stemmed: study, studi, study, good, better, car, automobil$

Word ordering

Consider these two sentences sentence:

I can't speak French, but I can speak English.

I can't speak English, but I can speak French.

Under our simple sentence embedding technique these two sentences have the same representation however they have the opposing semantics.

Some words contain more information than others

When analysing sentences and documents, some words contain more information than others. One of the simplest ways of dealing with this is using a TF-IDF (Term Frequency, inverse document frequency). This weights words according to how often they appear within a sentence or document (term frequency), but also weighs words that occur rarely across sentences or documents higher.

$\text{tf-idf}(t, d) = \text{tf}(t, d) \times \text{idf}(t)$ where $\text{tf}(t,d)$ is the frequency of term $t$ in document $d$

$\text{idf}(t) = \log \frac{1 + n}{1 + df(t)} + 1$ where $\text{df}$ is the inverse document frequency

from nltk.corpus import stopwords
from nltk.stem import WordNetLemmatizer
from nltk.stem import PorterStemmer 

def data_preprocessing(unittext, stop_words=stopwords.words("english"), lemmatizer=WordNetLemmatizer() ):
    # We assume it's already lowercase and cleaned.
    
    tokens = nltk.word_tokenize(unittext)
    if "lemmatize" in dir(lemmatizer):
        unittext = [lemmatizer.lemmatize(word) for word in tokens]
        stop_words = [lemmatizer.lemmatize(word) for word in stop_words]
    elif "stem" in dir(lemmatizer):
        unittext = [lemmatizer.stem(word) for word in tokens]
        stop_words = [lemmatizer.stem(word) for word in stop_words]
    else:
        return("No method 'stem' or 'lemmatize' in lemmatizer")
    
    unittext = [word for word in unittext if word not in stop_words]
    
    unittext = " ".join(unittext)
    
    return unittext

Extending the model to address identified flaws¶

We've addressed some of our flaws however there's to main ones that we haven't addressed well

  • Semantic meaning of words
  • Words are used contextually

The reason why we started with word embeddings is because word embeddings play the most important role in similarity analysis (as well as alot of other types of NLP modelling). If we get better representation of word vectors, we get better comparisons between vectors.

We'll cover two main types of models here:

  • LDA embeddings
  • Context-free embeddings

LDA¶

We won't cover this in detail. General steps are

  • Train an LDA model to distinguish between topics
  • Use the model to obtain distributions for each focument
  • Compare the topic distributions to each other using a variety of distance metrics:
    • Hellinger
    • Kullback-Leibler
    • Jaccard
  • Compare document similarities using
    • Shannon-Jensen divergence
    • Kullback-Leibler

Word2Vec (Context-free learning models)¶

Learning based methods, in simple words, use neural networks to learn word representations. These have been studied in depth and are around a decade or two old.

Common context-free learning based word embeddings include

  • Word2Vec (Google)
  • GloVe (Stanford)
  • fastText (Facebook)

They differ slightly so I'll explain the concept behind word2vec. There are three concepts behind word2vec

  1. Learning based
    • A neural network (auto-encoder like) is used to learn word representation
  2. Continuous bag of words (CBOW) or Skipgram
    • Theses are two architectures of how Word2Vec embeddings are learned
    • CBOW uses a window around a word to predict that word, Skipgram uses a word to predict the surrounding words
    • One of these assumptions is the distributional hypothesis, which is the idea that the meaning of a word can be understood from the words that tend to be near it. For example bread might tend to show up near eat, bake, butter, toast, etc., and this entourage gives a signal of what bread means.
  3. One word - One embedding

With word2vec models we can do stuff like king - man + woman = queen with it's embeddings

Document embeddings can be obtained through various means

  • you can naively average or add up all word2vec vectors (Bag of words)
  • you can include a document vector to be trained in the word2vec training process, this is called Distributed Memory - Paragraph Vector (PV-DM)
  • you can train paragraph vectors by using them to predict words within the paragraph, this is called Distributed Bag of Words - Paragraph Vector (PV-DBOW)

Universal Sentence Encoder (Attenion-based learning models)¶

We wanted to explore the use of attention-based learning methods, these are only a few years old! However, we don't have the compute power to start using these models to their full potential, training them on VM's will also be very costly.

There is one key takeaway from attenion-based learning: Embeddings for the same word changes based on the context it's used in

The reason why these models are called "attenion" based models (after the paper "Attention is all you need") is because these models learn the relative importance of context words when creating word embeddings. That is, the model learns to "pay attention" to what words are important when understanding the word at hand.

A simple example can be seen by studying the sentence: I used to live in France, I can speak fluent ___________. A human can clearly pick up that the missing word is "French" by paying attention to the word "speak" and "France". These NLP models try and mimic this type of human understanding of words

Limitations and reflections our analysis¶

There is one main limitation that we haven't found any resources on how to resolve, that is, similarity metrics are hard to compare between different pairs of documents.

As a really simple VET related example,

Is "Sell to retail customer" and "Sell products and services" more similar to each other, or is, "Apply knowledge of WHS laws in the workplace" and "Apply knowledge of WHS legislation".

With most non-NLP similarity metrics it's quite easy to compare but for document similarity models, it's easy to compare the similarity of a range of documents to one specific document (e.g. Google does this every day), but it's hard to compare the similarity of different pairs of documents.

For our use case, a ranking of similarity is not enough and we unfortunately have to deal with this issue.

Good news! We can steal someone elses word embedding model (sorta)¶

Word embeddings are hard to get right and very computationally expensive if you want to take into account all the factors that make language unpredictable. In comes transfer learning.

The idea behind transfer learning is that you take someone elses already learned word embeddings (e.g. Stanford, Google, Facebook models) and you can adapt those word embeddings to words that appear in your data i.e. you can essentially take an awesome model and customise if for your use case.

The caveat here is that transfer learning only works well if it's used for a supervised task. This is because you need some sort of a loss or scoring function to back-propogate the weights in order to "relearn" the word embedding model.
